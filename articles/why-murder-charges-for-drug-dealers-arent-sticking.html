First-degree murder charges for drug dealers aren't sticking. Here’s why.

Florida prosecutors have leveraged a 2017 state law to pursue first-degree murder charges against drug dealers, but convictions remain elusive.
By Hannah Phillips May 20, 2022

Something's off, said Christian Hernandez. The heroin tasted funny.

He took the drug while his girlfriend watched over Skype. She told him not to finish it, she said, but he didn't listen. His parents found him dead in his bedroom the next morning.

They learned later that the powder was cut with fentanyl, a synthetic opioid 50 times more potent than heroin. Christopher Massena, the man who sold Hernandez the fatal dose fatal dose

"I will pray for you every day," she told him. "But I'll also have peace knowing that you will not destroy another family like you have destroyed mine."

Lawmakers ask who's to blame for a fatal overdose

The federal prosecution of Massena in 2016 was the first of its kind in Palm Beach County, and it pointed to a growing rift between old and new schools of thought playing out in courtrooms across the United States: Can drug dealers be blamed for another person's overdose?

At one end of the debate are grieving families who want someone to pay for their loved one's death, their comments memorialized in court transcripts across the state.

"Give him the longest possible sentence," said John Sterople, the brother-in-law of Maggie Crowley, a Wellington woman who overdosed on counterfeit oxycodone pills in 2016. "Give him the longest possible sentence so that he can experience a small fraction of the loss that we've been asked to bear."

At the other end are advocates who say that imprisoning street-level dealers does nothing to staunch the flow of drugs in America, and may even deter people from calling 911 when they witness an overdose. Lawmakers are caught somewhere in the middle.

One year after a federal judge sentenced Massena to 30 years in prison, Florida Gov. Rick Scott signed a bill expanding the state's homicide statute and strengthening state attorneys' prosecutorial powers in cases involving fentanyl. Under the new law, dealers could face murder charges if their customers fatally overdose on the drug.

Supporters of the change argued that as overdose rates continued to climb, so too should the potential penalty for anyone deemed responsible.

Palm Beach County State Attorney Dave Aronberg promised in 2018 to use the bill to "aggressively charge drug dealers who spread deadly fentanyl-laced heroin," and he indicted a handful of people on first-degree murder charges. If successfully convicted, the charge carries a potential punishment of life in prison or the death penalty.

Shalonda Golden was among the first. She was indicted for first-degree murder in the 2017 fatal overdose of Travis Tekel, but was sentenced to just three years in prison after she pleaded guilty to manslaughter. Vicki Sakers was indicted on first-degree murder in the 2017 overdose death of Candace Moreland; she also pleaded guilty to the lesser charge of manslaughter.

So did Joreel Sine, who was sentenced to 12 years in prison for manslaughter this month.

"It really was a weak case," said Ed Reagan, who defended Sine against the state's homicide charge. "It was a weak case, or they would have gone forward with first-degree murder."

Five years have passed since the state imposed stricter penalties for fentanyl-related drug offenses, and not one indictment by the Palm Beach County State Attorney's Office has ended in a successful conviction of first-degree homicide. As defendants instead accept plea deals for charges with a fraction of the prison-time, overdose deaths in the county keep climbing.

"Fentanyl was a growing menace then," Aronberg said. "And it has, in fact, surpassed even our worst expectations."

The path from accidental deaths to premeditated murder

Decades before Scott signed the bill expanding Florida’s homicide law to include fentanyl, adults convicted of “death by drug distribution” risked spending 20 years to life in prison under the Controlled Substances Act of 1986.

It didn't happen often, Aronberg said. The challenge in prosecuting those cases was the same then as it is now – proving beyond a doubt where the fatal dose came from. People with substance-use disorders often have "multiple drug dealers on speed dial," he said. How do you tie a bad batch of drugs to one specific dealer?

Usually, you don’t. Without a witness to point fingers, or incriminating text messages left on the victim’s phone, assistant state attorney Diva O'Bryan said overdoses don't often lead to an arrest for murder.

Those that do face another hurdle if they make it as far as a trial by jury. Jack Fleischman, the West Palm Beach attorney who represented Massena in federal court, said jurors don't always have the same attitude toward the victim of a fatal overdose as they do the victim of a homicide.

Why doesn't the decedent bear some responsibility? he asked. Why only the person who's alleged to have delivered the drug?

A letter written from Massena's friend to the judge prior to sentencing argued as much.

"I'm a heroin addict. I have been for 10 years," the friend wrote. "If I go out right now and buy heroin, it's my fault. No one held a gun to my head and made me do it."

None of the state's cases has gone to trial. Reagan, who defended Sine against first-degree homicide charges, said that going before a jury would have been too much of a gamble.

"These cases are so, so punitive," he said. "It's not like playing a card game, where you're going to lose a couple hundred bucks. If a jury convicted him of the first-degree murder, he's going to lose the rest of his life."

Instead, Sine will spend 12 years in prison for his role in the drug deal that killed Candace Moreland. A face mask covered his mouth when the judge read the sentence, Reagan said, hiding his expression.

Federal prosecutors have had more success convicting dealers of first-degree homicide. A federal judge in 2018 sentenced Johnny Clyde Benjamin to life in prison for giving fentanyl-laced pills to 34-year-old Maggie Crowley. She overdosed on the pills and died on Sept. 1, 2016.

Opinions vary as to the impact of lengthy prison sentences on dealers. Aronberg said he believes that the threat of a life sentence and the death penalty has had a hand in scaring would-be dealers out of the market.

"The message has gotten out," he said. "It's hard to know how many deaths that prevented, but I think it's important that we have such a law on the books."

To Peggy Hernandez, the difference is nominal.

"Regardless of how long they go away for – five years, six years, 10 years, 20, 30," she told the Palm Beach Post on Thursday. "My son's still dead. My son's not coming back."

According to data collected by the Florida Medical Examiners Commission, fentanyl-related overdose deaths have not slowed, despite legislators' efforts to target drug dealers.

"If you're looking to law enforcement and prosecutors to end the opioid epidemic through laws like this, I think that's only part of the solution," Aronberg said. "And it may only be a small part of the solution."

Aronberg is a prosecutor, he said, but he's also a realist. He knows you can't arrest your way out of a drug problem. The approach has to be holistic.

That means getting Narcan into the hands of first responders and high-risk civilians, he said, and supporting measures like the county's Sober Home Task Force, which targets sober homes that take payment to refer addicts to treatment centers. The task force has made 121 arrests since its inception in 2016, though the first person charged with patient brokering was found not guilty in 2021 on all 119 counts against him.

Angie Hatfield, whose 28-year-old daughter, Brianna Oberly, died of a suspected drug overdose in February, is on a mission to get Narcan in as many hands across the state as possible.

Brianna Oberly, 28, died of a suspected drug overdose six months after being released from prison.Courtesy of Angie Hatfield

Oberly was just six months out of prison herself when she died in a hotel room, her mother said. Hatfield carries a notebook filled with the details she's learned about her daughter's death: Who was with her when it happened, the sounds she made while she died.

"I would love to have vengeance," Hatfield said. "But it wouldn't bring her back. Giving somebody a death sentence for this doesn't make it better."

Saving someone else's life might.
