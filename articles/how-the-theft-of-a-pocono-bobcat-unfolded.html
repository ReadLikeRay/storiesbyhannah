How the theft of a Pocono bobcat unfolded, as told by the accused

The thief, a 45-year-old musician with a soft spot for animals, left chunks of hair, a claw and $50 behind.
By Hannah Phillips Feb. 17, 2022
Blanche the 22-year-old bobcat was taken from Pocono Snake and Animal Farm on Feb. 4, 2022.Courtesy of Pocono Snake and Animal Farm

"Pray for me," Samuel Levit said before his court hearing. "Please, pray."

He sounded tired. Levit became the subject of public scrutiny two weeks ago after police say he stole a bobcat from a zoo in the Poconos, leaving chunks of hair, a claw and $50 behind. A zoo employee arrived minutes after the theft to find the garage door open, and Blanche the bobcat missing.

"This man walked in and out four minutes later with a 22-year-old bobcat in his arms," said Jon Lowris, whose family has owned the Pocono Snake and Animal Farm for decades. "I don't know if I could have done it in four minutes. But that's what he did."

Images of Levit and his red Hyundai were pulled from a Ring security camera and shared widely across social media. Readers played a game of whodunnit in the comment sections, quick to ridicule — who steals a bobcat? — and speculate — was it an inside job? It wasn't, Lowris would say later.

Hours passed without an arrest, and the lore around the man grew. He's an animal-rights vigilante, some guessed, or an exotic animal trafficker, a Pennsylvanian Tiger King.

In truth, he's a 45-year-old musician with a soft spot for animals. Levit has frequented the zoo since he was a teenager, he said. He visited with his mother during trips from Staten Island to the Poconos, and he remembers even then seeing the cat behind the cage. He isn't convinced that it's a bobcat — more like a mountain lion with some lynx blood, he said over FaceTime.

Levit leafed through a large book, one he said he could fill with all the violations committed by the owners of Pocono Snake and Animal Farm. A review of animal welfare inspections between 2015 and 2021 found that the zoo violated USDA standards on five occasions, all which were deemed non-critical by inspectors.

Levit worried over the animals anyway, concerned that they were being mistreated. He imagined them locked in cages feet away from the prey they're meant to hunt, and the thought made him sick. In his home, Blanche would be surrounded only by guitar amps, family photos and a wary calico cat named Daisy.

Bobcats are worth as much as $5,000, but Levit didn't know that, he said. He thought the $50 would suffice, and he drove her home, respectfully. He said that word, "respectfully," again and again as he spoke about his afternoon with Blanche. He checked her for injuries and fed her cold cuts and milk.

Back in East Stroudsburg, troopers searched for a red Hyundai, and people online wished the worst for the man with the cat. Slowly, Levit began to realize that something was wrong. News of a theft at the zoo made it back to him, but that didn't make any sense, he said; "I'm not a thief."

He learned something terrible then: bobcats cost a lot more than $50. Suddenly afraid, Levit said he packed Blanche into his trunk and started to drive. A state trooper spotted him around 5 p.m., parked at the zoo for the second time that day. He was there to return the cat, Levit told the trooper.

Blanche sat in an animal carrier in the trunk, a chain and carabiner fitted tightly around her neck. She was stressed, Lowris said, but she didn't appear to have any serious injuries. Levit was arrested and charged with 12 felony, misdemeanor and simple charges related to animal cruelty and theft.

"He said that he just wanted to feed the bobcat," a trooper wrote after he interviewed Levit. "He provided no further information."

He was jailed for two days before returning home on bail. His cat Daisy had run low on food by then, and so had he, until his mother stopped by with enough for the both of them. His name and face appeared in posts across social media as word of the arrest spread.

“I read the Facebook comments. People had a good old time with this,” said James Fuller, a Monroe County public defender. “But I think this is a story about someone who’s very mentally disturbed.”

Levit was wary of speaking to the Pocono Record reporter who called and asked about his day with Blanche. He hung up initially, then called back five times, speaking each time with more distress and urgency about the charges against him. He called a final time Wednesday morning, hours before his preliminary hearing.

"Pray for me, please," he said.

He was quiet in front of the judge, speaking just once to correct the public defender when he mispronounced his name. Noreen Lowris, owner of the Pocono Snake and Animal Farm, watched over Zoom.

Fuller and Levit ultimately decided to waive the preliminary hearing and pursue a mental health evaluation. He could face up to 20 years in prison if he's found guilty of burglary in the first degree.

"We hope Mr. Levit gets the help that he needs," Noreen Lowris said Wednesday afternoon. "We wish him the best for the future."
