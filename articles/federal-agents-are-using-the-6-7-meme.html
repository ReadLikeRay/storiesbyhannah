Federal agents are using the 6-7 meme to catch suspected child predators

The dying meme finds new life in West Palm sex-trafficking stings.
By Hannah Phillips Feb. 3, 2026
A federal criminal arrest report details undercover officers' efforts to use the viral '6-7' meme to identify child predators.Hannah Phillips/The Palm Beach Post

As children across the country declare the viral "6-7" meme dead, the nonsensical phrase has found new life among federal agents in South Florida.

According to arrest reports filed in West Palm Beach, undercover officers with Homeland Security posted fake advertisements of a teenage girl on a commercial sex website and listed her age as 67 — a deliberate reference, one agent said, to the viral trend.

“The use of the age 67 was intended by law enforcement to reference a common theme and meme on social media: ‘Six / Seven’ that trended with teenagers and children,” the officer wrote in a Feb. 1 report.

Several men responded to the ads, though it’s unclear from court records whether they understood the 6-7 reference. Officers also used words like “petite” and “fun sized” to describe the fictitious girl, whose face was concealed but appeared “clearly much younger than 67,” according to an officer.

Men inquired about the girl’s age. Undercover agents said she was 15.

"That's a child. Are you crazy? No good, no go. Bye," responded 45-year-old Shannon Jermaine Hollingsworth of Greenacres.

Despite his initial disinterest, agents say Hollingsworth agreed to meet the teen for sex the following day. Officers arrested him upon arrival, unmoved by his claim that he'd planned to report the girl to police for prostitution.

Asked why he brought a condom to the encounter, Hollingsworth said "he is a man and always has condoms on him," an agent wrote. Hollingsworth added that he is "good-looking and does not need to pay for sex."

Hollingsworth is represented by the Federal Public Defender's Office which, as a practice, does not comment on open cases. He remains in custody.

He was one of four men arrested between Jan. 30 and Feb. 1 in connection with the fake ads. All were charged with attempted sex trafficking of a child, which carries a penalty between 10 years and life in prison.

The meme that helped ensnare them originated from rapper Skrilla's 2024 song "Doot Doot (6 7)." Kids shouted it for the better part of 2025 at the mention of the numbers six or seven, or for no reason at all. Named Dictionary.com's 2025 Word of the Year, and so disruptive it was banned in some schools, it means essentially nothing.

By the time of the local sex-trafficking arrests, the meme had already begun to fade. Google Trends data showed searches peaked in late October and have been declining since.

Federal law-enforcement agencies have long struggled to master internet culture and lingo. In 2014, the FBI released an 83-page internal glossary of nearly 3,000 abbreviations, acronyms and shorthand the agency said was popular among young internet users.

The Washington Post called it "hilariously, frighteningly out of touch." It included entries like ALOTBSOL ("always look on the bright side of life"), PMYMHMMFSWGAD ("pardon me you must have mistaken me for someone who gives a damn"), and IITYWTMWYKM ("if I tell you what this means will you kiss me.")
