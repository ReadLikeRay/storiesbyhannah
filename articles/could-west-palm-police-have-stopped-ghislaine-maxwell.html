Could West Palm Police have stopped Ghislaine Maxwell in 2001?

The first agency tipped off about Jeffrey Epstein said it wasn't their case to investigate. Legal experts disagree.
By Hannah Phillips March 2, 2026
Ghislaine Maxwell and Jeffrey Epstein appear in an undated photo released as part of the Epstein Files Transparency Act.Provided by the Justice Department

After learning Ghislaine Maxwell was recruiting "young, beautiful, unmarried women" from Palm Beach Atlantic University to work at Jeffrey Epstein's home, West Palm Beach police referred the matter elsewhere and pursued the case no further.

They had no choice, said police spokesperson Rachel Leitão. It was out of their jurisdiction.

Legal experts disagree. In interviews with The Palm Beach Post, attorneys said Maxwell's recruiting of college students on the West Palm Beach campus — reported in 2001, three years before what was believed to be the first tip to police — was a crime the department had every right to investigate.

"I just don't see how anybody could suggest otherwise," said Bob Jarvis, a law professor at Nova Southeastern University's Shepard Broad College of Law.

Jarvis said that while Epstein's conduct on the island was indeed the purview of Palm Beach police, Maxwell's efforts to lure girls there was itself a violation of the law. Whether the agency would have known to pursue it as such when sex-trafficking laws were still in their infancy is another matter.

How a 2001 tip on Epstein slipped through the cracks

Florida law prohibits soliciting another person to commit prostitution, regardless of whether a sex act occurs. The story Maxwell told the students — that she was hiring them for administrative work, to answer phones — doesn't change the analysis, Jarvis said.

"You can call a hippopotamus a zebra, but that doesn't make it a zebra," Jarvis said.

"The law doesn't give you a defense if you lie. That statute is very broadly written. Whatever she was doing there, there's just no way for her to slip out of it."

In fact, he added, the deception ran afoul of a second law. The federal Trafficking Victims Protection Act, enacted in 2000, outlawed recruitment for commercial sexual exploitation through fraud.

Terry Coonan, a law professor at Florida State University who served as an expert witness in a civil lawsuit brought against Maxwell by Virginia Giuffre, said Maxwell's conduct fit squarely within the TVPA's fraud prong.

"What Maxwell did was arguably an act of fraud — telling those young women that something very different was going to be expected of them once they got to the Epstein residence," said Coonan, who founded FSU's Center for the Advancement of Human Rights and has trained law enforcement for the Justice Department and Florida Attorney General's office.

The TVPA was one year old when the PBA students told West Palm Beach police Epstein touched them inappropriately, which means trainings for state and local law enforcement had only just begun. Coonan said even within the legal and human-rights communities, the prevailing assumption was that the law applied primarily to foreign-born victims. It wasn't until around 2008 that advocates recognized it covered U.S. citizens, too.

"I wouldn't point a finger too quickly at them," he said of the West Palm Beach police.

Jarvis agreed that the department's failure to refer the case to the FBI was more a missed opportunity than a dereliction. An agreement among Palm Beach County agencies and federal investigators made cooperation possible but did not require it.

"Lots of mistakes were made by lots and lots of people," Jarvis said. "We'll never know every person who was responsible for letting this slide."

West Palm Beach police, for their part, have not said whether officers weighed these statutes, consulted the State Attorney's Office or considered calling the FBI.

"As previously stated," Leitão said in an email to The Post, "the West Palm Beach Police Department’s jurisdiction is limited to incidents occurring within the city limits of West Palm Beach."

It's impossible to say what impact an investigation by West Palm Beach police would have had on Epstein's case, but the record offers some clues.

When Palm Beach police began investigating Epstein in 2005, Maxwell's name didn't come up as a potential co-conspirator — in part because the girls she originally recruited were by then recruiting other girls themselves.

"Once you get the first few victims, the victims bring other victims," Coonan said. "You can really kind of sit back."

A 2001 investigation into Maxwell might have interrupted that cycle before it compounded. Instead, when Palm Beach detectives built their case against Epstein in 2005 and 2006, they did so without knowing Maxwell had been flagged years earlier.

That failure was bigger than any one police department, Jarvis said. He pointed to missed opportunities, institutional blind spots and a reluctance to believe people of Epstein and Maxwell's social standing were capable of such depravity.

"You just wouldn't expect these people to be doing what they did," Jarvis said. "We can talk all day long about whether we're too hands-off with celebrities. We tend to believe the best about them until they show us otherwise."

Maxwell wasn't charged until 2020, one year after Epstein was found dead in his jail cell. Convicted in 2021, she is serving a 20-year sentence.
