If Cuba falls, DeSantis hopes exiles leave Florida, rebuild island

The governor said he hopes those who fled to Florida decades ago will return to the island to 'create a potentially prosperous life.'
By Hannah Phillips March 26, 2026
Gov. Ron DeSantis appears at Palm Beach Atlantic University in March 2026.Photo by Hannah Phillips/The Palm Beach Post

Gov. Ron DeSantis said Florida has a plan to manage a surge of Cubans into the state if the island's government collapses, but he hopes it doesn't come to that.

Speaking March 25 before an invite-only crowd at Palm Beach Atlantic University, DeSantis said the prospect of a regime change sending migrants across the Florida Straits has long been a concern. His preferred outcome is the reverse: Cuban exiles in Florida returning to the island to help rebuild it.

"The Trump administration agrees with us," he said. "I would love to see some of the folks here, who grew up in Cuba and had to flee, go back and help create a potentially prosperous life."

Nearly one million Cuban exiles live in Florida, with the largest concentration in Miami-Dade County. DeSantis did not say what it would mean for the region if a significant portion of that population, built over six decades of successive exile waves, returned to the island.

He also offered no details on what Florida's contingency plan involves if the flow runs the other direction.

The governor's comments came as Cuba faces what some analysts describe as its worst economic crisis since the fall of the Soviet Union.

The Trump administration cut off oil shipments to the island after the U.S. operation that removed Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, Cuba's primary oil supplier, in January. The island has suffered three major nationwide blackouts in the past four months, and its electrical grid — built around Soviet-era infrastructure — has become increasingly unreliable.

Trump has said a regime change in Cuba would be "next" after the U.S. war against Iran concludes, telling Latin American leaders at Mar-a-Lago this month that "Cuba's at the end of the line."

DeSantis predicts post-regime Cuba becomes a honeymoon destination

DeSantis said a free Cuba would not need much to succeed — only basic rule of law and a government that isn't "100% corrupt" — and predicted the island would quickly become a destination for American tourists. He said he saw the Cuban coastline firsthand as a naval officer stationed at Guantanamo Bay and found it "very pretty."

"Your kids and grandkids will be going on spring break there," he said. "They'll be going on honeymoons there. They'll be doing golf trips."

That vision, he said, first requires removing the Castro-era government, which he called a "cancerous tumor" at the heart of the Western Hemisphere.

"A pro-Western, pro-America free government in Cuba would be a boon for that island," he said. "Now, how we get there has been very tricky over the years."

The governor pointed to the 2021 anti-government uprising in Cuba, which saw authorities cut internet service across the island to suppress protests sparked by food and medicine shortages. DeSantis said he negotiated with a private company to fly hot-air balloons over the island and broadcast internet access to civilians, but had to abandon the plan once the Biden administration threatened the company's federal business license.

"I thought that was a huge missed opportunity to be able to help those people seize their own destiny and move in a direction of freedom," he said, echoing Trump's message to Iranians following the death of Iranian Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei.

DeSantis did not say whether he has resumed efforts to aid the island under the Trump administration.
