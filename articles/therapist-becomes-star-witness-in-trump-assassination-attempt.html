Therapist becomes star witness in Trump assassination attempt trial

"You’re an American hero," said Ryan Routh to the man whose keen ear and quick thinking thwarted his escape. “I celebrate you.”
By Hannah Phillips Sept. 12, 2025
Lawyers and onlookers leave the Alto Lee Adams Sr. Courthouse in downtown Fort Pierce.Photo by Crystal Vander Weit/The Palm Beach Post

Attorneys prosecuting Donald Trump’s would-be assassin could hope for no better witness than Tommy McGee.

The therapist was on his way to buy office furniture when he heard gunshots at Trump International Golf Club on Sept. 15, 2024. A U.S. Secret Service agent had just opened fire at Ryan Routh, the man accused of plotting to kill Trump as he golfed.

McGee had grown up in a neighborhood plagued by gunfire. He told jurors on Sept. 11 that he’d seen cases of gun violence go unsolved thanks to wary witnesses unwilling to share details with police. Having already driven past the golf course, he made a U-turn and headed back.

Routh, a 59-year-old North Carolina man who prosecutors say drove 800 miles to Florida with hopes of killing Trump, sprang out of the bushes surrounding the golf course. He looked panicked, McGee said, and frantic, like he wanted to get away from “whatever he’d just done.”

Routh and McGee locked eyes. While Routh sprinted across the street to his Nissan Xterra, McGee said he scrambled for his phone. He wanted to photograph Routh in case it would be helpful to police, he said, but he didn’t want to be obvious about it. He couldn’t open his camera app in time.

McGee pulled into a driveway along the side of the road, intent on photographing the running man, the car he sprinted to and its license plate. He took several photos but couldn’t get a clear shot of the plates as Routh backed up and pulled away, he said.

McGee shifted back into drive and followed him. He reached for the pen and paper he used to take notes during his clients’ therapy sessions and wrote Routh’s plate number. He wrote it again, several times over, on clean sheets of paper in case he needed to distribute them to officers.

“Are you in the habit of stopping and taking down license plates?” asked Assistant U.S. Attorney John Shipley.

“No,” he answered. “I was trying to buy some furniture.”

Officers, skeptical of therapist, later used his help to make arrest

He circled back to the golf course instead, unsure what he would find. It was already swarming with armed men and women in uniform, officers from agencies he recognized and others he didn’t. They’d begun to block off the area and “didn’t look too happy” to see McGee’s approach, he said.

McGee said he waved an officer over to his car, lowered his window and asked whether they were investigating a shooting. The officer said nothing. If they were, McGee continued, he had photos of the person who might have done it, as well as his license plate number.

McGee was calm. His friends often remarked on his ability to keep a level head in tense situations, he said, as well as his hypervigilance — two qualities without which Routh might have fled unnoticed. Of all the drivers who passed during the shooting and the moments that followed, McGee was the only one who stopped.

Officers were surprised and skeptical, McGee said, but they quickly began to take him seriously. He gave a description of the man — tall, blonde, white — and his clothes, the car he left in and the direction he went. He handed over the pages with his license-plate number, holding onto the original, which had notes from a recent therapy session on it.

Soon, deputies with the Palm Beach County Sheriff’s Office told McGee that Martin County deputies had stopped a person of interest along Interstate 95. Would he be willing to fly there, in a PBSO helicopter, and look at the man?

McGee agreed. By that point, he still did not know that it was a suspected assassination attempt, nor that Trump was its target.

Routh asks McGee his political leanings during cross-examination

On the helicopter ride over, McGee saw where traffic along the highway appeared backed up. At its head was a black Nissan Xterra and a tall blonde man in a salmon-colored shirt. Deputies couldn’t find his ID, but a passing FBI agent — on his way from Martin County to Mar-a-Lago in response to the suspected assassination attempt — stopped and offered deputies his portable biometrics device. With it, they identified the man as Ryan Routh.

Deputies had McGee look at Routh carefully. It looked like the same man, McGee said. He asked deputies to turn him to the side, then said more emphatically: Yes, it looks like him. He said he was “99.9%” certain.

Prosecutors asked McGee to identify Routh in court. He pointed to him and described the gray suit jacket he wore. Accused of trying to assassinate a then-major presidential candidate, Routh, representing himself, faces life in prison.

“You’re the man,” Routh told McGee when given the chance to cross-examine him. Nearly every question and observation drew objections from prosecutors.

“Are you a Trump supporter?” Routh asked McGee, who is Black. “Do you think Madea, Beyoncé and Michelle Obama are going to be mad?”

U.S. District Judge Aileen Cannon told the jury to disregard the questions and rebuked Routh, who concluded his cross-examination thereafter.

“You’re an American hero,” he said before returning to the defense table. “I celebrate you.”
