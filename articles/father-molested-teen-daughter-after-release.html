'Unspeakable': Father molested teen daughter after release from prison for infant's death

Peter Zayas lost custody of his eldest daughter in the wake of his infant's death. When they reunited years later, investigators say he abused her, too.
By Hannah Phillips June 4, 2024

With his release from prison imminent, Peter Zayas had a rare opportunity: Atone for the death of his infant daughter by becoming a father to his teenage one. Instead, he victimized her, too.

Zayas began sexually abusing his 16-year-old daughter within a week of his release from prison in 2022. He moved into the home she shared with her adoptive parents in Palm Beach County and told her to call him Peter, not Dad. He gave her a cellphone with which to send him explicit photos of herself and assaulted her in secret, in parking lots before school.

"I'm a prisoner to your love," Zayas texted his daughter in March 2023. "Keep me in chains baby."

Palm Beach County sheriff's deputies arrested him three weeks later. He'd deleted their texts, but deputies retrieved more than 3,400 pages of messages from his daughter's phone. Sent during the eight months between Zayas' release from prison and entry to jail, they chronicled the fast work he made of dashing the paternal image she'd built of him throughout his incarceration.

Zayas, 39, pleaded guilty to sexual battery and incest in exchange for a 30-year prison sentence on Wednesday. A spokesperson for the Palm Beach County State Attorney's Office said the nature of the crime was so horrific that attorneys recited the details to Circuit Judge Jeffrey Gillen in private, to keep other inmates — who Zayas might encounter some day in prison — from hearing.

Zayas showed less caution than the lawyers. During a different sentencing hearing in January, he reiterated his desire for his daughter in the same breath that he apologized for it.

"I am going to be full-on and outwardly honest with you guys," he told U.S. District Judge Aileen Cannon, who oversaw a federal criminal case stemming from the same abuse. "I loved her."

Cannon sentenced Zayas to life in prison, rejecting a bid for mercy by Assistant U.S. Public Defender Scott Berry. Berry suggested that because a New Mexico court terminated Zayas' parental rights in 2010, he wasn't technically the girl's father. The defense attorney called him her "paramour" instead.

He said the two had not seen one another since she was a toddler and therefore "had never had an actual father-daughter relationship." Berry outlined in court filings the gruesome set of circumstances responsible for their estrangement.

Zayas was a sergeant in the U.S. Air Force in 2007 when his wife murdered their infant daughter. Having left the child alone with a violent and alcoholic mother, Zayas took partial blame for her death.

He pleaded guilty to negligent child abuse in 2014, resigning himself to more than a decade in prison and forfeiting custody over his eldest daughter. She was adopted under a new name and welcomed into a Palm Beach County home in the aftermath of his arrest. She wrote poetry and excelled in school.

The girl connected with her biological father for the first time at the tail end of his prison sentence. She told investigators she was excited to build a relationship with him, and he seemed eager for their reunion, too. Secretly, he said, he was asking God to take away the feelings he had for her. They remained.

Zayas told Cannon that he wasn't blind to "that troubling factor — she is your daughter, she is your daughter." He said the need to keep their relationship hidden and the constant reminder that he couldn't be with her like a "regular woman” drove him nearly to suicide.

In a recorded statement played aloud for the judge, his daughter said she'd tried to hurt herself, too. She said she felt manipulated, humiliated and guilty for her father's arrest. Her adoptive parents described their own guilt in letters to the judge. One asked Cannon, a mother of two, to think of her own children when sentencing Zayas.

"Envision the torment etched across her face, the pain that no parent should ever have to witness in their child," the girl's mother wrote. "As a mother, as a protector, how would you feel knowing that the one you hold closest to your heart has been violated in the most despicable manner?"

There is criminal wrongdoing, Cannon said afterward, and then there is depravity. Zayas' actions fell within the latter.
