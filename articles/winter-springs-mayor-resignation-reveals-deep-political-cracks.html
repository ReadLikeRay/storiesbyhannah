A mayor’s resignation reveals deep political cracks in this Florida city

“I had to be the face of a very rude, very arrogant, very power-hungry group of people,” Charles Lacey said. “I could no longer proudly represent my city.”
By Hannah Phillips March 22, 2021

Winter Springs mayor Charles Lacey announced his resignation March 12 with a year and eight months left to serve. It was too much to endure, he said. He couldn’t do it.

“I had to be the face of a very rude, very arrogant, very power-hungry group of people,” Lacey said. “I could no longer proudly represent my city.”

Lacey’s rise and fall from office is rife with accusations of cronyism and political favors, some hurled by the mayor and others hurled at him. To Lacey’s supporters, the story has one clear villain, and he works on the city commission.

“There’s one leading the pack, and what he wants to do gets done,” said Tom Foster, who has lived in Winter Springs for almost 30 years. “Everybody else falls in line.”

Others describe a mayor who fell out of touch with the community and is skulking as a result. Some predict Lacey is just one casualty in a growing struggle between old and new schools of thought playing out across Seminole County.

“Lines are being drawn between different political viewpoints, and it’s going to collide at the next election,” said city manager Shawn Boyle. “It is not a Winter Springs issue. It’s a county-wide issue.”

Lacey didn’t think his time as mayor would end this way. He was elected in 2010 and said his first two terms were “nothing but a joy.” He oversaw tax cuts, led a hands-off government and said he was proud of the community that was born from it.

“I had a feeling that everywhere I went, there was sort of a glimmer of respect, a glimmer of envy in people’s eyes,” Lacey said. “Everyone was content.”

That changed in 2018, he said. Political opponents stirred dissent among the community and amplified problems he didn’t consider problematic. Lacey and then-incumbent commissioners were called to answer for too much traffic, too much pavement and too few trees around Winter Springs.

“To their credit, these politicians did it well,” Lacey said. “The entire commission was turned upside down over the course of two elections.”

The campaign against overdevelopment ushered in a new wave of officials during the 2018 and 2020 elections, and Lacey watched the rift between him and them grow.

They didn’t share the same hands-off governing approach he felt was most successful, Lacey said. They were over involved, and their “wish list of things they want to do” went far beyond what he thought any government’s should.

“They believe every problem is something that the city needs to solve,” Lacey said. He doesn’t.

Among Lacey’s biggest concerns was commissioner Kevin Cannon: the power he seemed to hold over the group and the lack of independent thinking exercised by the remaining commissioners.

“We have four commissioners who — because they’re intellectually lazy, or because they’re physically lazy, or because they’re just not engaged in the process — choose to abdicate their authority to one commissioner,” Lacey said.

He believes the commissioners vote as a bloc according to what Cannon wants. The commission votes to fill positions like city manager, city attorney, city clerk and mayor, Lacey said, so those officials “live in complete subjugation to one man.”

“Complete and total fealty to Kevin Cannon is the most important characteristic in choosing a commissioner,” Lacey said. “Not the quality of the candidate, not independent thinking.”

It’s a damning conspiracy, and one that has bled into the public view.

“There’s a lot of antagonism between him and the commission,” said Jesse Phillips, vice president of the Winter Springs Community Association. “Particularly Mr. Cannon, who is kind of the most influential commissioner.”

Tom Foster, former president of the Winter Springs Rotary Club, said he has seen it too.

“We got a commissioner who has the longer-standing commissioners voting along his preferences,” Foster said. “It appears this gentleman got the two new commissioners in his back pocket as well.”

Cannon denied the charges against him and his colleagues.

“It is impossible for any one commissioner to have absolute power,” he wrote in an email.

Cannon said the commissioners rarely have a difference of opinion and work collectively to problem solve and reach a consensus, then vote. They approve motions with a unanimous vote ‘yes’ a majority of the time, he said, but not all the time.

The city’s explanation for Lacey’s departure is simpler than the mayor’s.

“His pro-development stance does not align with the current Commission and general community’s desire to maintain Winter Springs as the charming small town it has been known for since its founding,” the city said in a statement following Lacey’s resignation announcement March 12.

Development has been a source of contention for Lacey; he faced scrutiny in 2019 for vetoing an ordinance that would temporarily halt residential development. Still, he denied being pro-development and said the city’s statement is a “fantasy” designed to portray him in a negative light.

Not everyone is convinced.

“I consider Charles a friend of mine,” said city manager Shawn Boyle. “I think he’s stretching a little bit.”

Ken Spalthoff, a Winter Springs resident who ran unsuccessfully against Lacey in 2018, called the mayor’s resignation “dereliction of duty,” and his portrayal of Cannon absurd.

“He didn’t like the way the commission wasn’t playing ball with him on development, and he quit. That’s plain and simple,” he said. “That’s how it happened.”

Monday is Lacey’s last official day as mayor, and the logistics of his vacancy will be discussed at 6:30 p.m. in Winter Springs City Hall.

The city commissioners will vote on a new mayor in April. Lacey said he prays that Cannon makes a good choice.

“I very much fear for how that decision can go because there are very wrong choices to be made,” Lacey said. “But at the end of the day, the city is really no worse off than they are now. It’s still one man calling the shots.”
